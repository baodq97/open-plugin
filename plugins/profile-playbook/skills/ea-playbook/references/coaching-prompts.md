# Coaching Prompt Templates

Inline coaching templates organized by SFIA skill and level transition. Use these as starting points — adapt to the specific context.

## Format

```
> **EA Coach** ({SKILL_CODE} L{current} \u2192 L{target})
> {Observation about what was just done or is about to be done}
> {Actionable tip for performing at the next level}
```

## When to Coach

- After completing a significant artifact
- When an architecture decision is made
- At phase transitions
- When the user asks "why" about a step

## When NOT to Coach

- Every single step (causes fatigue)
- During routine data entry
- When the user is in flow and didn't ask

## Enterprise and Business Architecture (STPL)

### L5 \u2192 L6

**After creating a capability model:**
> **EA Coach** (STPL L5 \u2192 L6)
> Good development of models and plans to drive business strategy execution.
> At level 6, go further: develop enterprise-wide architecture and processes that embed strategic change management. Lead the systems capability strategy review, not just contribute to it. Set strategies and policies to ensure compliance between business and technology strategies.

**After creating a roadmap:**
> **EA Coach** (STPL L5 \u2192 L6)
> The roadmap effectively guides business strategy execution.
> At level 6, develop roadmaps for enterprise architecture initiatives with active stakeholder buy-in. Capture and prioritize market trends and alternative strategies. Develop business cases for high-level initiative approval and funding.

### L6 \u2192 L7

**After enterprise-wide architecture review:**
> **EA Coach** (STPL L6 \u2192 L7)
> Strong leadership in developing enterprise-wide architecture.
> At level 7, direct the development of enterprise-wide architecture that embeds strategic change. Oversee the creation of roadmaps for long-term enterprise transformation. Ensure compliance between business strategies, transformation activities and technology directions.

## Phase Transition Coaching

### Discover \u2192 Define

> **EA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Discovery complete. Key output: {summary of business strategy, current state, and drivers identified}.
> Reflection: Did you engage management-level stakeholders, not just operational ones? Enterprise architecture requires strategic alignment from the top. Moving to Define — translate these findings into architecture vision and principles.

### Define \u2192 Design

> **EA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Architecture defined. Key output: {architecture vision, principles count, capability model scope}.
> Reflection: Does every principle trace to a business driver? Aspirational principles without rationale become shelfware. Moving to Design — create reference architectures and roadmaps that realize the target state.

### Design \u2192 Govern

> **EA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Architecture designed. Key output: {summary of reference architectures and roadmap}.
> Reflection: Are the reference architectures actionable for delivery teams? Architecture that only architects understand is architecture that won't be followed. Moving to Govern — establish the structures that ensure adherence.

### Govern \u2192 Evolve

> **EA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Governance established. Key output: {governance model, ARB charter, compliance criteria}.
> Reflection: Is your governance enabling or blocking? The best governance makes the right path the easy path. Moving to Evolve — ensure the architecture adapts and improves continuously.

### Session Complete

## Business-IT Alignment Coaching

Particularly important coaching — helps the EA maintain strategic focus. Use when the user drifts into solution-level detail.

**When the user focuses on implementation details:**
> **EA Coach** (STPL L5)
> This level of detail is important but belongs in solution architecture, not enterprise architecture. EA defines the direction, standards and governance. Ask yourself: "Does this decision affect one solution or the entire enterprise?" If one solution, delegate to the SA.

**When the user creates architecture without business traceability:**
> **EA Coach** (STPL L5 \u2192 L6)
> Every architecture decision must trace to a business capability or strategic objective. Before finalizing, verify: "Which business capability does this architecture serve? Which strategic objective does it advance?" If you can't answer, the decision needs more context.

**When the user over-governs:**
> **EA Coach** (GOVN L6)
> Note: governance enables, not blocks. If the governance overhead discourages delivery teams from engaging with architecture, it's too heavy. The test: "Would a delivery team voluntarily seek architecture review?" If not, simplify.

## EA vs SA Boundary Coaching

Coaching on responsibility boundaries — helps new EAs understand the limits of their scope.

**When the user starts designing specific solutions:**
> **EA Coach** (STPL L5)
> Note: solution-level design is SA scope, not EA scope. The EA provides: (1) reference architectures and standards, (2) architecture principles, (3) governance and review. The SA designs specific solutions within those guardrails.

**When the user asks "How should we implement this?":**
> **EA Coach** (STPL L5)
> Great question, but implementation is owned by delivery teams. The EA answers: "What are the architectural guardrails?" and "Does this solution conform to our reference architecture?" Implementation decisions (technology choices within guardrails, detailed design) belong to the SA and delivery team.
