# Coaching Prompt Templates

Inline coaching templates organized by SFIA skill and level transition. Use these as starting points — adapt to the specific context.

## Format

```
> **BA Coach** ({SKILL_CODE} L{current} → L{target})
> {Observation about what was just done or is about to be done}
> {Actionable tip for performing at the next level}
```

## When to Coach

- After completing a significant artifact
- When an analysis decision is made
- At phase transitions
- When the user asks "why" about a step

## When NOT to Coach

- Every single step (causes fatigue)
- During routine data entry
- When the user is in flow and didn't ask

## Business Situation Analysis (BUSA)

### L3 → L4

**After business investigation:**
> **BA Coach** (BUSA L3 → L4)
> Good investigation of the business situation and identification of key issues.
> At level 4, adopt a holistic view — look beyond the immediate symptoms. Conduct root cause analysis: "Why does this problem exist? What systemic factors, processes, or organizational dynamics contribute?" Map cause-and-effect chains, not just symptoms.

**After root cause analysis:**
> **BA Coach** (BUSA L3 → L4)
> Solid root cause analysis linking symptoms to underlying causes.
> At level 4, validate your findings with multiple stakeholder groups. The same problem looks different from operations, management, and customer perspectives. Cross-reference at least three viewpoints before finalizing your analysis.

### L4 → L5

**After comprehensive situation assessment:**
> **BA Coach** (BUSA L4 → L5)
> Thorough analysis of the business situation with clear recommendations.
> At level 5, advise on the approach itself — which analysis techniques fit this situation? Engage executive-level stakeholders, not just operational ones. Shape the investigation strategy, don't just execute it.

**After identifying systemic issues:**
> **BA Coach** (BUSA L4 → L5)
> Strong identification of systemic issues across multiple business areas.
> At level 5, translate these findings into strategic recommendations. Connect individual problems to organizational capability gaps. Your analysis should inform business strategy, not just project scope.

## Requirements Definition (REQM)

### L3 → L4

**After documenting requirements:**
> **BA Coach** (REQM L3 → L4)
> Good requirements documentation with clear structure and traceability.
> At level 4, actively facilitate stakeholder input — don't wait for requirements to come to you. Provide constructive challenge: "Is this requirement truly needed? What business outcome does it enable? What happens if we defer it?"

**After elicitation sessions:**
> **BA Coach** (REQM L3 → L4)
> Effective elicitation capturing stakeholder needs.
> At level 4, use multiple elicitation techniques per requirement area — interviews alone miss tacit knowledge. Combine workshops, observation, and document analysis. Cross-validate what stakeholders say they need against what they actually do.

### L4 → L5

**After establishing requirements baseline:**
> **BA Coach** (REQM L4 → L5)
> Good requirements baseline with stakeholder agreement and traceability matrix.
> At level 5, negotiate between stakeholders with competing priorities. Select and adapt requirements methods for the initiative's complexity. Drive prioritization decisions, don't just facilitate them. Own the requirements strategy.

**After managing requirements conflicts:**
> **BA Coach** (REQM L4 → L5)
> Strong facilitation of conflicting stakeholder requirements.
> At level 5, establish reusable requirements governance practices. Define how requirements changes are assessed, approved, and communicated. Build organizational capability in requirements management, not just project-level artifacts.

## Feasibility Assessment (FEAS)

### L4 → L5

**After feasibility evaluation:**
> **BA Coach** (FEAS L4 → L5)
> Solid feasibility assessment covering technical, operational, and economic dimensions.
> At level 5, challenge the feasibility criteria themselves — are you measuring the right things? Assess organizational readiness and change capacity, not just technical viability. Your feasibility report should influence go/no-go decisions at the portfolio level.

**After cost-benefit analysis:**
> **BA Coach** (FEAS L4 → L5)
> Good quantitative analysis of costs and benefits.
> At level 5, account for intangible benefits and opportunity costs. Model multiple scenarios (optimistic, realistic, pessimistic) and identify the assumptions that most affect the outcome. Decision-makers need to understand sensitivity, not just the base case.

## Business Process Improvement (BPRE)

### L4 → L5

**After process analysis:**
> **BA Coach** (BPRE L4 → L5)
> Thorough analysis of the current-state process with clear pain points identified.
> At level 5, benchmark against industry practices — not to copy, but to identify improvement patterns. Quantify process inefficiencies: time lost, error rates, rework costs. Your analysis should build an irrefutable case for change.

**After improvement recommendations:**
> **BA Coach** (BPRE L4 → L5)
> Strong improvement recommendations with clear rationale.
> At level 5, design the transition path, not just the target state. Address change management: who is affected, what skills need updating, what resistance should you anticipate? A brilliant process design fails without adoption planning.

## Data Modelling (DTAN)

### L3 → L4

**After documenting data requirements:**
> **BA Coach** (DTAN L3 → L4)
> Good documentation of data entities and their relationships.
> At level 4, think about data governance — who owns this data? How is quality maintained across sources? Model not just the structure but the lifecycle: how is data created, used, archived, and retired? This holistic view separates L4 from L3.

**After creating data models:**
> **BA Coach** (DTAN L3 → L4)
> Clear data model capturing key entities and relationships.
> At level 4, validate your model against real business scenarios. Walk through 3-5 actual use cases and verify the model supports them without awkward workarounds. Pay special attention to edge cases — they reveal structural weaknesses.

## Acceptance Testing (BPTS)

### L3 → L4

**After defining acceptance criteria:**
> **BA Coach** (BPTS L3 → L4)
> Good acceptance criteria covering the main functional scenarios.
> At level 4, ensure every criterion is testable and tied to a specific business requirement. Use the pattern: Given {context}, When {action}, Then {measurable outcome}. Also define negative test cases — what should the system reject?

**After creating test scenarios:**
> **BA Coach** (BPTS L3 → L4)
> Solid test scenarios covering the happy path.
> At level 4, systematically cover boundary conditions and exception flows. Ask stakeholders: "What would make you reject this solution?" Their answers become your most important test cases.

### L4 → L5

**After acceptance testing strategy:**
> **BA Coach** (BPTS L4 → L5)
> Strong acceptance testing approach with clear coverage.
> At level 5, define the acceptance testing methodology for the initiative — not just individual tests, but the overall validation strategy. Determine who participates, how UAT environments are managed, and what "done" looks like across all stakeholder groups. Drive organizational testing standards.

## User Experience Analysis (UNAN)

### L4 → L5

**After UX analysis:**
> **BA Coach** (UNAN L4 → L5)
> Good analysis of user needs and interaction patterns.
> At level 5, advocate for the user across all project decisions. Connect UX findings to business outcomes — poor UX is not an aesthetic problem, it is a productivity and adoption risk. Establish UX validation practices that persist beyond this project.

**After user journey mapping:**
> **BA Coach** (UNAN L4 → L5)
> Comprehensive user journey map capturing touchpoints and pain points.
> At level 5, use journey maps to identify cross-functional dependencies and organizational silos that degrade the user experience. Your UX analysis should inform process improvement and organizational design, not just interface design.

## Business Modelling (BSMO)

### L4 → L5

**After business model creation:**
> **BA Coach** (BSMO L4 → L5)
> Clear business model capturing key value streams and capabilities.
> At level 5, ensure the model is actively used for decision-making, not just documentation. Connect business models to measurable KPIs. Challenge the model: "If market conditions change, which parts break first?" A robust model anticipates disruption.

**After capability mapping:**
> **BA Coach** (BSMO L4 → L5)
> Strong capability map linking business functions to strategic objectives.
> At level 5, assess capability maturity and identify strategic gaps. Your model should answer: "What must we become to achieve our strategy?" This forward-looking view elevates business modelling from descriptive to prescriptive.

## Methods and Tools (METL)

### L4 → L5

**After methods selection:**
> **BA Coach** (METL L4 → L5)
> Good selection of analysis methods appropriate to the situation.
> At level 5, evaluate and tailor methodologies at the organizational level. Define when to use which technique, create reusable templates, and mentor others on method selection. Build a BA toolkit that scales beyond your own projects.

**After tooling decisions:**
> **BA Coach** (METL L4 → L5)
> Effective use of analysis tools for this engagement.
> At level 5, assess tool effectiveness across the BA practice. Recommend improvements, pilot new approaches, and establish standards. Your tool expertise should elevate the entire team's capability, not just your own productivity.

## Consultancy (CNSL)

### L4 → L5

**After stakeholder presentation:**
> **BA Coach** (CNSL L4 → L5)
> Good collaboration with stakeholders on this engagement.
> At level 5, take full responsibility for understanding client requirements — including data collection, analysis, and issue resolution. Manage the scope and delivery of the engagement to meet objectives. Ensure stakeholders can fully exploit the proposed solution.

**After delivering recommendations:**
> **BA Coach** (CNSL L4 → L5)
> Clear recommendations with supporting evidence.
> At level 5, anticipate stakeholder reactions and prepare for objections before presenting. Frame recommendations in terms of business value, not analytical rigor. The best analysis is worthless if stakeholders don't act on it.

## Stakeholder Management (RLMT)

### L4 → L5

**After stakeholder mapping:**
> **BA Coach** (RLMT L4 → L5)
> Comprehensive stakeholder map with influence and interest assessed.
> At level 5, develop differentiated engagement strategies for each stakeholder group. Anticipate coalition dynamics — who aligns with whom, where are the hidden blockers? Proactively manage relationships, don't just react to stakeholder requests.

**After managing conflicting interests:**
> **BA Coach** (RLMT L4 → L5)
> Effective navigation of competing stakeholder interests.
> At level 5, build trust-based relationships that survive project boundaries. Your stakeholder network should be an organizational asset. Mentor others on stakeholder engagement — this is one of the hardest BA skills to develop.

## Phase Transition Coaching

### Discover → Define

> **BA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Discovery complete. Key output: {summary of stakeholders, business context, and problem space explored}.
> Reflection: Did you speak with enough diverse stakeholders to avoid a narrow view of the problem? Were there perspectives you discovered late that changed your understanding? Moving to Define — formalize these findings into structured problem statements and requirements.

### Define → Analyze

> **BA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Problem and requirements defined. Key output: {count} business requirements, {count} stakeholder needs documented.
> Reflection: Are all requirements traceable to a business objective? Requirements without clear business rationale become scope creep later. Moving to Analyze — decompose these requirements and assess feasibility.

### Analyze → Design

> **BA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Analysis complete. Key output: {summary of key findings, feasibility results, process models}.
> Reflection: For each recommendation, did you consider at least 2 alternative approaches? Did you validate assumptions with data, not just stakeholder opinions? Moving to Design — translate analysis into solution specifications.

### Design → Deliver & Validate

> **BA Coach** (Phase transition)
> Solution designed. Key output: {summary of solution components, acceptance criteria, transition plan}.
> Reflection: Did you define acceptance criteria before the solution was built, not after? The BA ensures the solution is validated against business needs — the SA handles technical validation, the PO handles prioritization. Keep these responsibilities clearly separated. Moving to Deliver & Validate — verify the solution delivers promised business value.

## Root Cause Coaching

Particularly important coaching — helps the BA avoid jumping to solutions before understanding the problem. Use when the user short-circuits analysis.

**When the user proposes a solution before understanding the problem:**
> **BA Coach** (BUSA L4 — Root Cause)
> You've proposed a solution, but ask yourself: "Do I fully understand why this problem exists?" Jumping to solutions is the most common BA anti-pattern.
> Tip: Apply the "5 Whys" technique. State the problem, then ask "Why?" five times. If you can't answer confidently by the third "Why?", you need more investigation before proposing solutions.

**When the user self-challenges "Am I analyzing the right thing?":**
> **BA Coach** (BUSA L4 → L5)
> Excellent! Questioning your own analysis scope is a critical BA mindset. A simple test: "If I solve this problem completely, does the business outcome improve?" If the answer is unclear, you may be analyzing a symptom rather than the root cause. Step back and re-examine the problem hierarchy.

**When the user jumps to implementation details:**
> **BA Coach** (BUSA L3 → L4)
> You've moved into implementation territory — how the solution is built. The BA's focus is what the solution must achieve and why. Implementation details belong to the development team and SA.
> Tip: Reframe your statement as a requirement: instead of "We need a REST API that…", say "The system must enable {capability} so that {business outcome}." Let the technical team decide the how.

## BA vs SA/PO Boundary Coaching

Coaching on responsibility boundaries — helps BAs understand the limits of their scope and collaborate effectively with adjacent roles.

**When the user starts making architecture decisions:**
> **BA Coach** (CNSL L4)
> Note: technology and architecture decisions are SA scope, not BA scope. The BA provides: (1) business requirements — what the solution must do, (2) quality attributes — how well it must perform from a business perspective, (3) constraints — regulatory, organizational, or process limitations. The SA translates these into architecture. Collaborate closely, but respect the boundary.

**When the user starts making timeline/priority decisions:**
> **BA Coach** (RLMT L4)
> Note: roadmap timeline and feature prioritization are product decisions, not BA decisions. The BA provides: (1) requirements with business value assessment, (2) dependency analysis — which requirements depend on others, (3) impact analysis — what happens if a requirement is deferred. The PO decides: when to build, what priority, and what to cut. Support the PO's decision with evidence, but don't make it for them.

## Communication Coaching

Coaching on communication skills — essential for BAs who must bridge technical and business worlds.

**When presenting to the technical team:**
> **BA Coach** (METL L4)
> The technical team needs: precise requirements, clear acceptance criteria, data models, and process flows. Avoid ambiguous language — "the system should handle large volumes" is not a requirement. Specify: "The system must process 10,000 transactions per hour with less than 2-second response time." Use their vocabulary and reference architecture constraints.

**When presenting to business stakeholders:**
> **BA Coach** (CNSL L4 → L5)
> Business stakeholders do not need to know about data models or system interfaces. They need: what is the problem, how does it affect the business, what are the options, and what decision do they need to make. Lead with business impact, support with evidence, and end with a clear ask. Keep presentations under 15 slides with one key message per slide.

**When communicating across multiple audiences:**
> **BA Coach** (CNSL L4 → L5)
> Right approach! At L5, the BA must communicate effectively with both technical and business audiences. Same analysis, two narratives: detailed for the technical team (review thoroughly, challenge assumptions), summarized for business stakeholders (understand impact, make decisions). Tailor not just the content but the format — technical teams prefer documents they can study, executives prefer presentations they can react to. This is a critical Consultancy skill.
