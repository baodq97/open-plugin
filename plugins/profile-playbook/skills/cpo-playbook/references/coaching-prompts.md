# Coaching Prompt Templates

Inline coaching templates organized by SFIA skill and level transition. Use these as starting points — adapt to the specific context.

## Format

```
> **CPO Coach** ({SKILL_CODE} L{current} → L{target})
> {Observation about what was just done or is about to be done}
> {Actionable tip for performing at the next level}
```

## When to Coach

- After completing a significant artifact
- When a portfolio investment decision is made
- At phase transitions
- When the user asks "why" about a step

## When NOT to Coach

- Every single step (causes fatigue)
- During routine data compilation
- When the user is in flow and didn't ask

## Product Management (PROD)

### L5 → L6

**After creating a product strategy:**
> **CPO Coach** (PROD L5 → L6)
> Strong product lifecycle management with clear strategic direction.
> At level 6, think portfolio: how do all products in the organization relate to each other? Create a product lifecycle management framework that all product teams can follow. Champion product management principles across the org, not just within individual products.

**After portfolio prioritization:**
> **CPO Coach** (PROD L5 → L6)
> Solid prioritization of the product portfolio.
> At level 6, align product management objectives directly with business objectives. Authorize the selection and planning of all product management activities. Initiate the creation of new products by identifying how developing or adapting products creates new opportunities.

**After product roadmap review:**
> **CPO Coach** (PROD L5 → L6)
> Well-structured roadmap with clear product positioning.
> At level 6, oversee the entire portfolio's delivery of customer value. Create frameworks that enable product teams to make good decisions independently. The best CPOs build systems, not just strategies.

### L6 → L7

**After portfolio strategy review:**
> **CPO Coach** (PROD L6 → L7)
> Strong portfolio oversight with clear alignment to business objectives.
> At level 7, lead the definition of the strategic management framework itself. Direct the creation and review of strategy at the highest level. Your role shifts from managing the portfolio to shaping how the organization thinks about products.

## Phase Transition Coaching

### Discover → Define

> **CPO Coach** (Phase transition)
> Discovery complete. Key output: {summary of market landscape, competitive position, customer segments}.
> Reflection: Did you validate your market assumptions with real data, or are they still hypotheses? At the CPO level, strategy grounded in assumptions is strategy built on sand. Moving to Define — translate these findings into a portfolio vision and measurable strategy.

### Define → Prioritize

> **CPO Coach** (Phase transition)
> Strategy defined. Key output: portfolio vision, strategy, {count} portfolio OKRs, governance framework.
> Reflection: Is your governance framework enabling fast decisions, or creating bureaucracy? At L6, governance should empower product teams, not slow them down. Moving to Prioritize — allocate investment and make portfolio trade-offs.

### Prioritize → Plan

> **CPO Coach** (Phase transition)
> Portfolio prioritized. Key output: prioritization matrix, investment allocation, innovation pipeline.
> Reflection: Did you have the courage to sunset underperforming products, or did you spread investment too thin? At L6, the hardest CPO skill is saying "we will stop investing in this." Moving to Plan — coordinate execution across the portfolio.

### Plan → Deliver & Learn

> **CPO Coach** (Phase transition)
> Plan complete. Key output: portfolio roadmap, dependency map, stakeholder alignment plan, GTM strategy.
> Reflection: Does your plan account for what happens when strategic bets fail? At L6, planning means building adaptability into the plan itself. Moving to Deliver & Learn — execute, measure portfolio performance, and iterate strategy.

## Market-Driven Strategy Coaching

Particularly important coaching — helps the CPO avoid making product decisions based on assumptions rather than evidence. Use when the user relies on intuition over data.

**When the user proposes strategy without market evidence:**
> **CPO Coach** (MRCH L5 — Market-driven strategy)
> This strategy is well-articulated, but ask yourself: "What market evidence supports this direction? What customer data validates these assumptions?"
> Tip: Every strategic decision should have a market evidence foundation: "We believe {direction} because {market data/customer evidence}. We will validate this when {leading indicator} moves by {amount}."

**When the user focuses on internal capabilities over market needs:**
> **CPO Coach** (BUSA L5 → L6)
> Good analysis of internal capabilities. But the best product strategies start outside-in, not inside-out. What does the market need? Then, what can we uniquely provide?

**When the user proposes expanding into a new market without research:**
> **CPO Coach** (MRCH L5 → L6)
> Exciting opportunity. Before committing investment, ensure the market entry is grounded in evidence: market size, competitive landscape, customer willingness to pay, and channel feasibility. At L6, you set the standard for evidence-based market decisions.

## CPO vs CTO/Engineering Boundary Coaching

Coaching on responsibility boundaries — helps product leaders understand the limits of their scope.

**When the user starts making technical architecture decisions:**
> **CPO Coach** (RLMT L5)
> Note: technical architecture is a CTO/engineering decision, not a product decision. The CPO provides: (1) product vision and market direction, (2) portfolio priorities and investment allocation, (3) customer requirements and quality expectations. The CTO/engineering team decides: how to build it, what technology to use, and what technical trade-offs to make.

**When the user asks "What technology platform should we adopt?":**
> **CPO Coach** (PROD L5)
> The CPO's role is to define WHAT the product portfolio should deliver and WHY, not HOW. Instead of choosing platforms, articulate the product requirements: "We need products that can scale to 10M users, integrate across the portfolio, and support rapid experimentation." Then partner with the CTO to determine the technical approach.

## Communication Coaching

Coaching on stakeholder communication at the executive level.

**When presenting portfolio strategy to the board:**
> **CPO Coach** (RLMT L6)
> The board needs: strategic direction, investment rationale, risk assessment, key metrics proving progress, and decisions they need to make. Keep it concise. Lead with the strategic narrative, support with data, end with the asks.

**When communicating portfolio decisions to product teams:**
> **CPO Coach** (RLMT L5 → L6)
> Product teams need: clear priorities, resource commitments, strategic context for "why," and autonomy within guardrails. Present portfolio decisions as enabling constraints, not mandates. The best CPOs set direction and remove obstacles.

**When aligning with the CTO/engineering leadership:**
> **CPO Coach** (RLMT L6)
> Product-engineering alignment requires shared context, not just shared roadmaps. Ensure the CTO understands the market and customer rationale. Ensure you understand the technical constraints and opportunities. The strongest CPO-CTO partnerships are built on mutual respect for each other's domain.
