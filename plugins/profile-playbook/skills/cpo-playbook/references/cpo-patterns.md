# Common CPO Patterns

Reference guide for evaluating trade-offs during product leadership decisions. Each pattern includes a strategic analysis: start with the approach that fits your context, evolve as the organization matures.

---

## 1. Portfolio Strategy: Platform-led vs Product-led vs Customer-led

### When to choose what

| Criteria | Platform-led | Product-led | Customer-led |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Best for | Organizations with multiple products sharing common capabilities | Organizations with strong individual products and clear market segments | Organizations where customer relationships drive revenue |
| Strengths | Shared infrastructure reduces cost, enables rapid product creation | Clear product ownership, fast decision-making, market-focused | Deep customer understanding, high retention, account-based growth |
| Weaknesses | Platform team becomes a bottleneck, products lose autonomy | Duplication of effort, portfolio fragmentation, weak cross-product synergy | Over-customization risk, hard to scale, platform investment neglected |
| Org structure | Platform teams + product teams | Independent product teams | Customer success + product teams |
| Best suited for | B2B SaaS with multiple products, infrastructure companies | Consumer products, diverse product portfolios | Enterprise sales, professional services, regulated industries |

### Strategic recommendation

**Start with:** Product-led — each product has clear ownership, market focus, and accountability. This is the simplest model and avoids premature abstraction.

**Upgrade path:** When multiple products share 60%+ of capabilities → Platform-led to reduce duplication and accelerate delivery. When customer relationships are the primary growth driver → Customer-led to maximize retention and expansion.

**Avoid:** Platform-led too early. Building a platform before you have multiple products that need it creates complexity without value. Let shared needs emerge from real products.

## 2. Innovation Model: Core/Adjacent/Transformational vs Horizon Model vs Continuous Discovery

### When to choose what

| Criteria | Core/Adjacent/Transformational | Horizon Model (H1/H2/H3) | Continuous Discovery |
|----------|------------------------------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Best for | Portfolio investment allocation | Long-term strategic planning | Ongoing opportunity identification |
| Time horizon | Annual budget cycle | 3-10 year vision | Weekly/monthly cadence |
| Risk management | Explicit risk allocation by category | Risk increases with horizon | Small bets, fast learning |
| Typical split | 70/20/10 | Varies by industry and maturity | Variable, discovery-driven |
| Best suited for | Mature organizations with portfolio budgets | Strategic planning, board communication | Agile organizations, startup culture |

### Strategic recommendation

**Start with:** Core/Adjacent/Transformational with a 70/20/10 split — provides clear investment discipline and forces explicit conversation about risk appetite.

**Upgrade path:** Combine with Continuous Discovery for the Adjacent and Transformational categories — use small experiments to validate opportunities before committing full investment. Use the Horizon Model for board-level strategic communication.

**Avoid:** Allocating 100% to core products. Every portfolio needs some investment in the future, even during difficult periods. A 90/10/0 split is a portfolio heading for irrelevance.

## 3. Go-to-Market: Product-led Growth vs Sales-led vs Partner-led vs Community-led

### When to choose what

| Criteria | Product-led Growth | Sales-led | Partner-led | Community-led |
|----------|-------------------|-----------|------------|--------------|
| Best for | Self-serve products, high-volume low-ACV | Enterprise, high-ACV, complex sales | Markets with established channel partners | Developer tools, platforms, open source |
| Customer acquisition | Users find and adopt on their own | Sales team identifies and closes | Partners bring customers | Community attracts and retains users |
| Cost structure | Low CAC, high volume | High CAC, high LTV | Shared CAC with margin to partner | Low CAC, high effort to build |
| Speed to market | Fast — users try before buying | Slow — long sales cycles | Medium — partner onboarding needed | Slow to build, fast once established |
| Control | High — product drives experience | High — sales controls relationship | Lower — partner controls touchpoint | Lower — community shapes perception |
| Best suited for | SaaS, dev tools, collaboration tools | Enterprise software, complex solutions | Geographic expansion, regulated markets | Infrastructure, developer platforms, API products |

### Strategic recommendation

**Start with:** Match the GTM model to the product's natural buying behavior. If users can get value without talking to sales → Product-led growth. If the purchase requires organizational buy-in → Sales-led. If the market has established channels → Partner-led.

**Upgrade path:** Most successful companies use a hybrid. Product-led growth for user acquisition → Sales-led for enterprise expansion → Partner-led for geographic reach. Community-led as a long-term moat.

**Avoid:** Forcing a single GTM model on all products. Different products in the portfolio may need different GTM approaches. The CPO ensures the right model for each product.

## 4. Organization: Functional vs Product-aligned vs Outcome-aligned vs Matrix

### When to choose what

| Criteria | Functional | Product-aligned | Outcome-aligned | Matrix |
|----------|-----------|----------------|----------------|--------|
| Best for | Small organizations, shared resources | Medium organizations with distinct products | Mature organizations focused on outcomes | Large organizations with multiple dimensions |
| Structure | PM, Design, Engineering as separate functions | Cross-functional teams per product | Cross-functional teams per outcome/mission | Dual reporting (function + product/outcome) |
| Strengths | Deep functional expertise, resource flexibility | Clear ownership, fast decisions, market focus | Focus on outcomes not output, customer-centric | Balances functional depth with product focus |
| Weaknesses | Coordination overhead, slow decision-making | Functional silos within products, duplication | Hard to define ownership, requires maturity | Complex, dual reporting confusion, slow |
| Team autonomy | Low | High | High | Medium |
| Best suited for | Pre-product-market-fit, <20 people | Growth stage, 20-200 people | Scale stage, 100+ people | Enterprise, 500+ people |

### Strategic recommendation

**Start with:** Product-aligned — gives each product clear ownership and empowers teams to move fast. This is the most common and well-understood model.

**Upgrade path:** When multiple products serve the same outcome → Outcome-aligned to focus on customer outcomes rather than product boundaries. When functional expertise becomes a bottleneck → add lightweight functional guilds (not full matrix).

**Avoid:** Matrix too early. Matrix organizations require sophisticated management processes to work. Without them, matrix becomes "everyone has two bosses and nobody makes decisions."

## 5. Metrics: North Star + Input Metrics vs OKRs vs Balanced Scorecard

### When to choose what

| Criteria | North Star + Input Metrics | OKRs | Balanced Scorecard |
|----------|--------------------------|------|-------------------|
| Best for | Product team alignment | Goal-setting and tracking | Executive and board reporting |
| Focus | Single metric that captures value delivery | Ambitious goals with measurable key results | Multi-dimensional view (financial, customer, process, learning) |
| Complexity | Simple (1 metric + 3-5 inputs) | Medium (3-5 objectives, 3-5 KRs each) | High (4 perspectives, multiple measures) |
| Cadence | Continuous monitoring | Quarterly setting, monthly review | Quarterly or annual review |
| Best suited for | Growth-stage, product-focused orgs | All stages, especially growth and scale | Enterprise, board-facing, multi-business |

### Strategic recommendation

**Start with:** North Star + Input Metrics for the portfolio level — one metric that captures the value the portfolio delivers to customers. Add 3-5 input metrics that the product teams can directly influence.

**Upgrade path:** Layer OKRs on top for quarterly goal-setting — OKRs connect the North Star to actionable quarterly goals. Add Balanced Scorecard when the board or investors need a multi-dimensional view of portfolio health.

**Avoid:** OKRs without a North Star. OKRs tell you what to aim for this quarter, but without a North Star, each quarter's goals may pull in different directions. The North Star provides the consistent strategic thread.

## 6. Product Decision-Making: Data-informed vs Data-driven vs Intuition-led

### When to choose what

| Criteria | Data-informed | Data-driven | Intuition-led |
|----------|--------------|-------------|--------------|
| Best for | Strategic decisions with incomplete data | Operational decisions with clear metrics | New markets, breakthrough innovation |
| Data availability | Partial — directional data available | Full — robust metrics and analytics | Minimal — no historical data exists |
| Decision speed | Medium | Fast (data provides clear answer) | Fast (leader makes the call) |
| Risk | Moderate — data reduces but doesn't eliminate uncertainty | Low — data minimizes guesswork | High — depends on leader's judgment |
| Best suited for | Most strategic portfolio decisions | A/B testing, feature optimization, pricing | Market creation, category definition |

### Strategic recommendation

**Default to:** Data-informed for most CPO decisions. Use data to reduce uncertainty, but acknowledge that strategic decisions always involve judgment beyond what data can tell you.

**Reserve data-driven for:** Operational decisions where the data is clear (pricing optimization, feature usage analysis, churn prediction).

**Reserve intuition-led for:** Breakthrough innovation where no data exists yet. But treat intuition-led decisions as hypotheses — validate them quickly with market evidence.

## General Principles: Strategic Product Leadership

### Questions Before Every Portfolio Decision

1. **Market evidence test:** "What market data or customer evidence supports this decision?"
2. **Portfolio coherence test:** "Does this make the portfolio stronger as a whole, not just this product?"
3. **Investment discipline test:** "Are we investing where we have the right to win?"
4. **Organizational capability test:** "Do we have the talent and organizational structure to execute?"

### Pattern

```
Market evidence → Portfolio strategy → Investment allocation → Execution → Measurement → Strategy refresh
     (discover)       (define)           (prioritize)          (plan)      (deliver & learn)
```

Do not build strategy on assumptions. Build strategy on market evidence and validated learning.
