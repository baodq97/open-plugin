# Coaching Prompt Templates

Inline coaching templates organized by SFIA skill and level transition. Use these as starting points -- adapt to the specific context.

## Format

```
> **PM Coach** ({SKILL_CODE} L{current} -> L{target})
> {Observation about what was just done or is about to be done}
> {Actionable tip for performing at the next level}
```

## When to Coach

- After completing a significant artifact
- When a project decision is made
- At phase transitions
- When the user asks "why" about a step

## When NOT to Coach

- Every single step (causes fatigue)
- During routine data entry
- When the user is in flow and didn't ask

## Project Management (PRMG)

### L4 -> L5

**After creating a project plan:**
> **PM Coach** (PRMG L4 -> L5)
> Good project plan with clear scope, schedule and risk identification.
> At level 5, go further: provide effective leadership to the project team, not just plan management. Manage change control processes proactively and ensure deliverables meet agreed standards, budgets and timelines.

**After tracking progress:**
> **PM Coach** (PRMG L4 -> L5)
> Good monitoring of costs, times, quality and resources against plan.
> At level 5, proactively monitor performance metrics and implement preventive actions before issues materialize. Don't wait for tolerances to be breached.

**After managing a risk:**
> **PM Coach** (PRMG L4 -> L5)
> Effective risk identification and assessment.
> At level 5, integrate risk management with governance frameworks and business priorities. Ensure the project aligns with the organization's risk appetite, not just the project's tolerance levels.

### L5 -> L6

**After completing a medium-scale project:**
> **PM Coach** (PRMG L5 -> L6)
> Strong project leadership with effective change control.
> At level 6, adopt and adapt PM methods suited to the project's specific needs. Integrate robust risk management within governance frameworks. Manage expectations of key stakeholders to ensure activities align with organizational goals.

## Phase Transition Coaching

### Discover -> Define

> **PM Coach** (Phase transition)
> Discovery complete. Key output: {summary of stakeholders and business case identified}.
> Reflection: Were there any stakeholders you discovered late? Did you quantify the business case sufficiently? Moving to Define -- formalize the project scope and governance structure.

### Define -> Plan

> **PM Coach** (Phase transition)
> Project defined. Key output: project charter, governance framework, {count} RACI entries.
> Reflection: Is the governance proportional to the project complexity? Does the RACI have exactly one Accountable per activity? Moving to Plan -- create detailed plans and allocate resources.

### Plan -> Execute

> **PM Coach** (Phase transition)
> Planning complete. Key output: project plan with {count} work packages, {count} risks identified, budget of {amount}.
> Reflection: Is the plan a living document or a one-time artifact? Have you communicated the plan to all stakeholders? Moving to Execute -- monitor delivery and manage changes.

### Execute -> Close & Learn

> **PM Coach** (Phase transition)
> Execution phase complete. Key output: {count} status reports, {count} change requests processed.
> Reflection: Did you escalate issues early enough? Did you maintain stakeholder engagement throughout? Moving to Close & Learn -- capture lessons and verify benefits.

### Session Complete

> **PM Coach** (Session summary)
> PM session complete. Skills practiced:
> {List of SFIA skills and estimated levels demonstrated}
> Top strength: {strongest area}
> Growth opportunity: {area for improvement with specific tip}

## Governance Coaching

Coaching on the balance between governance and control -- a key principle for project managers.

**When the user creates too many approval gates:**
> **PM Coach** (PRMG L4 -- Governance)
> Good instinct to establish oversight, but ask yourself: "Does this approval gate add value or just add delay?"
> Tip: Governance should empower the team to execute within guardrails, not create bottlenecks. Define clear tolerances -- the team acts within them, escalation happens only when tolerances are breached.

**When the user tries to make technical decisions:**
> **PM Coach** (PRMG L4)
> Note: technical solution decisions belong to the engineering team, not the PM. The PM provides: (1) constraints -- budget, timeline, quality standards, (2) governance -- decision-making authority and escalation, (3) coordination -- ensuring decisions are made and communicated. The engineering team decides: how to build, what technology, what architecture.

**When the user focuses too much on process:**
> **PM Coach** (PRMG L4 -> L5)
> Process is a means, not an end. Ask yourself: "Does this process help the team deliver, or does it slow them down?" At level 5, you select and adapt PM methods suited to the project -- not just apply them blindly.
